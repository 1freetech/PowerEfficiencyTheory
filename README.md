# PowerEfficiencyTheory
Power Efficiency Charts to help understand the value of bitcoin based on its physical progression in power (TH/s) and efficiency (J/TH/s).

Power Efficiency Theory (P Theory) is a framework for estimating the long term valuation trajectory of technological systems by measuring the combined progression of computational power and energy efficiency. The theory proposes that value emerges from the physical progression of a system, which is ultimately produced by energy and expressed through measurable outputs such as computational throughput. In systems such as Bitcoin mining hardware, this progression can be quantified through hashrate production and energy efficiency measured in joules per terahash (J/TH). As computational power increases and the energy required per unit of computation declines, the productive capacity of the system expands. Power Efficiency Theory models this expansion through a compounding relationship between power growth and efficiency improvement, suggesting that technological systems with increasing computational output and declining energy costs tend to experience long term increases in their underlying economic value.

Within this framework, market price is treated as a fluctuating expression of the system’s underlying physical progression rather than its primary driver. Power Efficiency Theory therefore separates two interacting layers of behavior. The first is the technological progression layer, which evolves through improvements in computational power and energy efficiency. The second is the market cycle layer, which introduces temporary deviations through liquidity cycles, sentiment, and macroeconomic shocks. Over long time horizons, the theory proposes that market value tends to converge toward the system’s expanding physical progression, while periodic market contractions represent temporary deviations from that trajectory rather than permanent disruptions of it.
